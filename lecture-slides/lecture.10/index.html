Dynamics of Correlation

  • Correlation describes how two variables move together
    ‚Üí When one changes, does the other tend to change too?

  • It shows direction (positive or negative) and strength (tight vs. loose pattern)

  • Examples

    • Density of Buildings vs. Population Density ‚Üí positive
    • Median Income vs. Poverty Rate ‚Üí negative
    • Household size vs. Shoe Size ‚Üí none

Dynamics of Correlation - ‚Äòshape of a relationship‚Äô

  • Correlation is the shape of a relationship, not its cause.

Dynamics of Correlation - Measurement

  • The Correlation Coefficient (r)
    • Measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables.
    • Operates as a ‚Äòunitless measurement‚Äô: how much two variables move together, regardless of scale.

Dynamics of Correlation - Measurement

  • Computed as standardized covariance
    • regular covariance can be any value, standardizing it allows for an easier comparison.
  • Range: ‚Äì1 ‚Üí 0 ‚Üí +1
    • +1 ‚Üí perfect positive
    • 0 ‚Üí no linear relationship
    • ‚Äì1 ‚Üí perfect negative

Dynamics of Correlation - Measurement

This is the formula for the Pearson correlation coefficient, often written as r. It measures how much two variables move together, relative to their individual variability:


\[ r = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sigma_X \,\sigma_Y} \]

Dynamics of Correlation - Measurement

Symbol Meaning Description
Cov(X, Y) Covariance between X and Y Measures how X and Y vary together
œÉ‚Çì Standard deviation of X How much X varies around its mean.
œÉ·µß Standard deviation of Y How much Y varies around its mean.

Dynamics of Correlation - Measurement

  • Covariance itself is unscaled ‚Äî it depends on the units of X and Y (e.g., inches √ó pounds).

  • Dividing by œÉXœÉY standardizes it.

  • The result is unitless, always between ‚Äì1 and +1, so we can compare correlations across different variables.

Dynamics of Correlation - Measurement

Interpretation

  • Magnitude ‚Üí strength (0.1 weak, 0.3 moderate, 0.5+ strong ‚Äî context-dependent)
  • Sign ‚Üí direction (positive/negative)
  • Significance ‚Üí testable via cor.test() in R (is r ‚â† 0 by chance?)

Dynamics of Correlation - Measurement

Proceed with Caution

Correlation quantifies association, but understanding requires visualization and context.

  • Correlation ‚â† causation
  • Nonlinear patterns may yield low r despite clear relationships
  • Outliers can distort correlation dramatically
  • Visual verification: always plot before trusting r.

Correlation - A Sample Workflow

Correlation analysis in R is a structured exploration:

1Ô∏è‚É£ Clean and organize input data ‚Üí
2Ô∏è‚É£ Choose the right method ‚Üí
3Ô∏è‚É£ Compute r-values ‚Üí
4Ô∏è‚É£ Visualize ‚Üí
5Ô∏è‚É£ Interpret responsibly.

üß≠ 1. Prepare Your Environment

  • Load the required packages:
    • tidyverse ‚Üí for data manipulation
    • ggcorrplot or corrplot ‚Üí for correlation heatmaps
    • broom ‚Üí for tidy outputs from cor.test()
    • ppcor ‚Üí for partial correlations (optional)
  • Set your working directory and verify file paths.

üìÇ 2. Read and Inspect Your Data

  • Import your dataset (read_csv() or read_excel()).
  • Confirm variable types and names.
  • Identify which variables are numeric and suitable for correlation.
  • Handle missing values early (decide between pairwise or listwise deletion).
  • Run histograms per variable to gauge skewness or non-normality in data.

üîç 3. Choose Your Correlation Type

Method Best For Notes
Pearson (r) Continuous, linear relationships Sensitive to outliers and non-normal data
Spearman (œÅ) Monotonic, ranked, or nonlinear patterns Robust to outliers; works with ordinal data

Always match your method to the structure of your data.

üßÆ 4. Compute Correlation

  • Create a correlation matrix across selected numeric variables using cor().
  • Set:
    • use = "pairwise.complete.obs" for exploratory EDA, or
    • use = "complete.obs" for stricter inference.
  • Optionally compute pairwise tests with cor.test() to get p-values and confidence intervals.

üìä 5. Visualize Relationships

  • Use:
    • ggcorrplot() or corrplot() for a heatmap of r-values.
    • pairs() or GGally::ggpairs() for scatterplot matrices.
    • ggplot2 for focused scatterplots (two variables) with trend lines and r annotations.

üß† 7. Interpret the Results

  • Look at:
    • Direction (positive / negative)
    • Strength (magnitude)
    • Statistical significance (p < 0.05)
  • Always interpret correlations in context ‚Äî association is not causation.

üß† 7. Interpret the Results

  • Check for:
    • Outliers influencing r
    • Nonlinear relationships that Pearson may miss
    • Underlying variables that may confound X and Y
  • Interpretation tip:
    • Strong |r| ‚âà 0.7‚Äì1 ‚Üí tight pattern
    • Moderate |r| ‚âà 0.3‚Äì0.6
    • Weak |r| < 0.3

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Pitfalls in Correlation Analysis

  • Correlation assumes that each observation is independent and drawn from a consistent process.
  • Urban data often break this rule ‚Äî observations are close together in space, time, and social context, which can create misleading or inflated correlations.

üß≠ 1. Spatial Autocorrelation

  • What happens: Nearby locations often share similar values (e.g., rent, pollution, building age).
    The same pattern that makes a city spatially continuous also means your data points are not independent.
  • Effect: Correlation coefficients can appear artificially high because values co-move simply due to proximity.
  • Check for:
    • Clusters on maps
    • Moran‚Äôs I, Geary‚Äôs C, or Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA)

üåÜ 2. Data Density and Repetition

  • What happens: Many urban datasets include duplicate or overlapping spatial units
    (e.g., multiple 311 calls for the same address, or repeated building entries).
  • Effect: Non-independent observations inflate correlation and reduce interpretability.
  • Mitigation:
    • Collapse to unique IDs (parcel, BIN, BBL, or address)
    • Summarize or average repeated measures per unit
    • Inspect temporal or locational clusters

üßÆ 3. Spurious Correlation

  • What happens: Two variables rise together over time or space due to a common underlying driver, not a real relationship.

  • Example: both rent and coffee shop density rise across NYC so a relationship seems reasonable; however, the controlling, significant factor is neighborhood affluence.

üìÖ 4. Temporal Alignment Issues

  • What happens: Correlating variables measured at different times (e.g., 2024 crime vs. 2020 income) introduces lag bias.
  • Mitigation:
    • Align time windows before computing r

üìà 5. Over-Interpretation of Significance

  • What happens: With large spatial datasets, nearly everything becomes ‚Äústatistically significant.‚Äù
  • Mitigation:
    • Focus on effect size (|r|) and spatial pattern, not just p-values
    • Visualize ‚Äî context matters more than raw statistics
